Pattern 01

It's an allocation problem wearing a headcount problem's clothes.

A team is convinced they need to hire. We count the hours instead, mapped task by task against every role. A chunk of the work is sitting with the wrong people. Move the work, hire no one. The math is what tells the two apart — and almost nobody does the math first.

In one 150-person company: roughly a quarter of the operations department's tasks were actually several tasks wearing one name, and about 20 "tasks" turned out to be entire job descriptions.

Pattern 02

Nobody can tell you what "done" means.

Ask three people when a task is finished and you get three answers, or you get the task read back to you. That's not sloppiness. That's the reason work bounces back to you for approval — there's no written standard for it to be checked against, so you ARE the standard.

Same company: around 96 completion conditions just restated the task itself.

Pattern 03

The same work is being done twice in two places.

Different departments, different names, same output. Neither team knows about the other. This one is invisible from the inside and obvious the moment everything is on one page — which is exactly why you can't read the label from inside the jar.

A brokerage's records: 81 duplicate-pair listings reconciled down to 47 real ones.

Will this work for me?

It works if you're founder-led, past roughly $15k a month, and the thing capping you is delivery rather than demand. If you're turning work away, re-doing your team's work, or you're the approval step on everything — yes.

It won't work if your problem is lead generation. This fixes capacity, not pipeline, and I'll tell you that on day one rather than take the money. It also won't work if you want someone to hand it to and never think about it again — this is the diagnosis, and you have to be willing to act on it.

What if you don't find anything?

In 132 businesses that hasn't happened once — but here's the honest version of the answer. If I map your operation and the work genuinely is allocated correctly, you get the document that proves it, which is worth having the next time you're deciding whether to hire.

What I won't do is manufacture a problem to justify the invoice. If the finding is small, I'll say it's small.

How is this different from ChatGPT?

You should ask that. Genuinely — if you can get a real answer for free, go get it.

Here's the only difference that matters. A model gives you an excellent generic answer. It has never seen your handoffs. It doesn't know your ops lead is quietly carrying two roles, or that work bounces back every time because one completion condition in week three was never defined. I've mapped 132 businesses and not one got a generic answer, because the finding is always in the specifics — and the specifics only exist inside your business.

Should I just hire an ops person instead?

Maybe. But you'd be hiring them into the same undocumented mess, which is why the last one didn't work. Nine times out of ten "we need to hire" turns out to be "we've allocated the work wrong," and the math is what tells the two apart.

If you do need the hire, this tells you exactly which role, carrying which tasks, and what has to be written down before they start. That's a cheaper way to find out than a bad hire and six months.

Won't a better tool fix this?

Tools don't make you organized. They make whatever you already have louder. Most of the stacks I audit have three tools doing the same job and none of them handing work to each other — that's a wiring problem, and buying a fourth tool doesn't fix wiring.

Shouldn't I be spending this on marketing?

If you're not at capacity, yes — go get leads, this isn't for you yet.

If you're already turning work away, more leads make it worse. You'd be paying to increase the pressure on the exact part that's breaking. Get the delivery side able to absorb it, then spend on marketing and actually keep what it brings.
